\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
In 2007, András Schubert published a paper titled ``Successive h-indices.''~\citeyearpar{parent} It begins:
\begin{quotation}
	Hirsch's excessively discussed h-index generated a long row of interpretations, applications and derivatives within a surprisingly short period. Symptomatically, Hirsch's paper in the \textit{Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America} became the second highest cited paper of the issue in which it had been published. In this note, attention is called to an apparently so far overlooked option: h-indices themselves may form the basis of a successive series of h-indices.

	Given, e.g., the h-indices of the researchers of an institute (which indices will be denoted by h\textsubscript{1} in what follows), an index, h\textsubscript{2}, of the institute can be determined: the institute has an index h\textsubscript{2} if h\textsubscript{2} of its N researchers have an h\textsubscript{1}-index of at least h\textsubscript{2} each, and the other $\mathrm{N}-\mathrm{h}_2$ researchers have h\textsubscript{1}-indices lower than h\textsubscript{2} each. The succession can then be continued, e.g., for networks of institutions or countries or other higher levels of aggregation.

	Since no sufficient data for testing the concept on the researcher-institute-country hierarchy were available, another example was chosen.
\end{quotation}

Fifteen years later, OpenAlex~\citep{openalex} launched a FOSS bibliographic catalogue complete with citation indexing, authors, fields, and institutions. Recent evaluations have found it to be a broadly suitable substitute for proprietary sources such as Scopus and Web of Science at the institutional level~\citep{alperin2024,culbert2025}, and we take it here as the dataset for which Schubert wished in 2007.

In the years between, successive h-indices attracted modest but sustained attention. Egghe~\citeyearpar{egghe2008} gave the concept a theoretical footing, showing how the exponent of the underlying Lotka distribution propagates through each successive aggregation step. Two case studies followed shortly after: Ruane and Tol~\citeyearpar{ruane2008} computed a rational (non-integer) successive h-index across economics departments in Ireland, and Arencibia-Jorge et al.~\citeyearpar{arencibia2008} applied Schubert's exact researcher--department--institution hierarchy to a Cuban research institute using Web of Science data. More recently, Mryglod et al.~\citeyearpar{mryglod2022} cautioned that departmental- and institutional-level h-indices are driven largely by group size rather than quality, a critique we return to in Section~\ref{subsec:methods-supplementary}. None of this prior work, however, has had access to a comprehensive, open, institution-labeled snapshot of the kind OpenAlex now provides.

Schubert suggested that h\textsubscript{2} of institutes would be more interesting than that of journals; Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes how we compute it at scale, and Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Data source and filtering}
The underlying data is OpenAlex's public authors snapshot~\citep{openalex}, distributed as Parquet on \texttt{s3://openalex/data/parquet/authors/} (\texttt{us-east-1}, no credentials required) and partitioned into over 1,600 daily \texttt{updated\_date=} directories totalling about 53\,GB. Only four columns are read from each remote file (\texttt{id}, \texttt{h\_index}, \texttt{affiliations}, \texttt{topics}), and an author is retained only if all of the following hold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{summary\_stats.h\_index} is non-null and greater than zero;
    \item at least one entry in \texttt{affiliations} has \texttt{institution.type = 'education'};
    \item at least one entry in \texttt{topics} carries a non-null field classification.
\end{itemize}
Applying these filters to the full snapshot yields 27.1 million authors, spanning 20,907 institutions and OpenAlex's current list of 26 top-level fields. Because \texttt{s3://openalex/data/parquet/authors/} is a live, continuously-updated snapshot rather than a versioned release, these counts (and every downstream number in this paper) reflect its state as of our most recent pull; re-running the pipeline against a later snapshot state will not reproduce them exactly; we return to the size of this effect in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}. One limitation carried over from the source data: an author's \texttt{h\_index} is computed by OpenAlex from all works meeting its citation criteria regardless of retraction status, which OpenAlex records inconsistently~\citep{hauschke2025}; a small, unquantified fraction of h\textsubscript{1} values in our dataset may therefore be inflated by since-retracted work.

\subsection{Author-level assignment}
Each retained author is reduced to a single row: one institution and one field. An author may list several affiliations with \texttt{institution.type = 'education'}; rather than fractionally splitting or duplicating that author across institutions, we assign them to the one with which they were most recently affiliated. Recency is read from OpenAlex's \texttt{affiliations} field, which records, for each institution an author has ever listed, the calendar years in which they published while there; we take the institution whose most recent such year is latest, breaking ties (identical latest year) by the numerically smallest OpenAlex institution identifier. We deliberately do not use the similarly-named \texttt{last\_known\_institutions} field for this: despite its name, it is simply the set of affiliations listed on an author's single most recent work, carrying no per-entry recency of its own, so when an author has more than one such affiliation (e.g., a joint appointment or co-affiliated coauthors misattributed to one author) there is no way to tell which is ``more recent'' than another.

Field assignment works the same way but is weighted. OpenAlex tags each author with a set of topics, each carrying a \texttt{count} (roughly, the number of the author's works classified under that topic) and a parent field. We sum \texttt{count} within each field and assign the author to whichever field carries the largest total weight: their modal field by publication volume, not simply their single most granular topic. The result is an \texttt{authors} table with one row per author: \texttt{(author\_id, h\_index, institution\_id, institution\_name, field, field\_name)}.

\subsection{The h-index and its successive generalization}
Hirsch's~\citeyearpar{hindex} original index, here $\mathrm{h}_1$, is computed by OpenAlex per author from citation counts and taken as given. Schubert's generalization~\citeyearpar{parent} lifts the same definition up a level of aggregation: a group of $N$ members, each already carrying an $\mathrm{h}_{k-1}$ index, has an index $\mathrm{h}_k = n$ if $n$ of its members have $\mathrm{h}_{k-1} \geq n$ and the remaining $N-n$ members have $\mathrm{h}_{k-1} < n$:

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Successive h-index of a group $G$, given a value $v(m)$ for each member $m \in G$}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Sort $G$ by $v$ descending; let $r(m)$ be $m$'s 1-indexed rank in that order
\State \Return $\max \{\, r(m) : v(m) \geq r(m),\ m \in G \,\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

We use this rank-based procedure, implemented as a window function in SQL ranking rows within each group and taking the largest rank whose value has not yet fallen below it, for every successive index in this paper: $\mathrm{h}_2$ of an institution over the $\mathrm{h}_1$ of its authors, and $\mathrm{h}_3$ of a country over the $\mathrm{h}_2$ of its institutions.

\subsection{h\textsubscript{2}: institution and institution$\times$field}
h\textsubscript{2} is computed twice, over different groupings of the same author-level data. The field-specific table, \texttt{h2\_by\_institution\_field}, groups authors by (institution, field) and yields 328,500 pairs; institutions are matched on their numeric identifier only; institution and field \emph{names} are resolved afterward via \texttt{arg\_max}, so that spelling or formatting variants of the same name across snapshot partitions cannot silently split one institution into several groups. The institution-overall table, \texttt{h2\_by\_institution}, ignores field entirely and applies the same procedure across all of an institution's authors regardless of discipline, yielding one h\textsubscript{2} per institution (20,907 rows).

\subsection{h\textsubscript{3}: country level}
Institutions are mapped to countries using the \texttt{country\_code} field of OpenAlex's institutions snapshot (also read directly from S3, joined on institution ID). h\textsubscript{3} is then computed with the same rank-based procedure, once globally per country over the h\textsubscript{2} values of its institutions (\texttt{h3\_by\_country}), and once per (country, field) over the field-specific h\textsubscript{2} values (\texttt{h3\_by\_field/}), producing one file per field.

\subsection{Supplementary metrics}
\label{subsec:methods-supplementary}
Several secondary statistics are derived from the h\textsubscript{2} and h\textsubscript{3} tables to probe what the headline numbers obscure:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gini coefficient}, computed on the within-field distribution of institutional h\textsubscript{2}, quantifies whether a field's excellence is concentrated in one institution (Gini near 1) or broadly distributed (Gini near 0).
    \item \textbf{Efficiency} normalizes for scale, directly addressing the group-size dependence flagged by Mryglod et al.~\citeyearpar{mryglod2022}: $\mathrm{h}_2/\text{author\_count}^{1/\beta_1}$ at the institution level, and $\mathrm{h}_3/\text{institution\_count}^{1/\beta_2}$ at the country level. Both exponents come directly from Egghe~\citeyearpar{egghe2008}: his eq.\ 11 gives $\mathrm{h}_2 = \text{author\_count}^{1/(\alpha_0\alpha_1)}$ and his eq.\ 18 gives $\mathrm{h}_3 = \text{institution\_count}^{1/(\alpha_0\alpha_1\alpha_2)}$, where $\alpha_0,\alpha_1,\alpha_2$ are the Lotka exponents of the citation-per-article, article-per-author, and author-per-institution processes respectively, so we write $\beta_1 = \alpha_0\alpha_1$ and $\beta_2=\alpha_0\alpha_1\alpha_2$ for the two compound exponents the efficiency metrics actually need. Egghe's own theorems (his eqs.\ 7 and 14) show that $\beta_1$ and $\beta_2$ are themselves the Lotka tail exponents of the h\textsubscript{1}-across-authors and h\textsubscript{2}-across-institutions distributions, so rather than assume a value for either, we fit both directly by discrete maximum likelihood (Clauset et al.~\citeyearpar{clauset2009}, via the \texttt{powerlaw} package~\citep{alstott2014}, with the tail threshold $x_{\min}$ chosen automatically by minimizing the Kolmogorov--Smirnov distance to the fitted distribution), giving $\beta_1=1.833$ (fit on all 27.1 million authors' h\textsubscript{1} values) and $\beta_2=2.917$ (fit on all 20,907 institutions' h\textsubscript{2} values). $\alpha_2$, the Lotka exponent of authors-per-institution, is fit the same way directly from the author\_count column ($\alpha_2=2.021$), and $\alpha_1$, the exponent of articles-per-author, is fit from each author's OpenAlex \texttt{works\_count} ($\alpha_1=2.996$) after excluding 46 of 27,083,177 authors with \texttt{works\_count}$>10{,}000$: every one of these pairs an implausible publication count (up to 278,862) with a single-digit h\textsubscript{1}, the signature of an OpenAlex author-disambiguation failure (a generic name or collaboration byline merged into one author ID) rather than genuine output. $\alpha_0$, the citation-per-article exponent, is fit directly from a sample of 718,299 works with nonzero citations drawn from 25 OpenAlex works-snapshot partitions spread across its full 2016--2026 date range (rather than the complete $\sim$725~GB snapshot, since a single $\sim$360K-row partition already yields an order of magnitude more tail data than a stable MLE fit requires), giving $\alpha_0=2.742$.

    Taken at face value, these four independently-fit exponents are inconsistent with Egghe's own model: $\beta_1/\alpha_1=0.612$ and $\beta_2/(\alpha_1\alpha_2)=0.482$ both imply $\alpha_0<1$, which his model rules out by construction (eq.\ 4 requires every Lotka exponent to exceed 1 for the underlying population to be finite), and the predicted $\beta_2=\beta_1\alpha_2$ comes out 21\% off the observed $\beta_2$. Section~\ref{subsec:model-consistency} traces this to global pooling across all 26 OpenAlex fields: refitting the same four exponents within a single field brings the implied $\alpha_0$ to $0.981$ for Physics and Astronomy, essentially at the boundary the model requires, while the analogous $\beta_2$ vs.\ $\beta_1\alpha_2$ mismatch does not shrink at all within a single field, evidence of a genuine break specifically at the institution-aggregation step rather than an artifact of heterogeneity. We report both exponents and both findings rather than silently picking whichever normalization makes the efficiency metric look best justified.
    \item \textbf{Breadth score} counts, per institution, how many of the 26 fields it ranks in the global top 10, top 50, and top 100.
    \item \textbf{Non-biomedical ranking} recomputes institution-level h\textsubscript{2} after excluding authors whose modal field is Medicine, Biochemistry/Genetics/Molecular Biology, Immunology and Microbiology, Neuroscience, or Health Professions, to test whether the overall ranking is, in substance, a ranking of medical school size.
    \item \textbf{Academic Olympics} converts each field's country-level h\textsubscript{3} ranking into an Olympic-style medal table: the top three distinct h\textsubscript{3} values in a field are gold/silver/bronze, ties at the same h\textsubscript{3} share a medal and the next tier is skipped (as in the real Olympics), and medals are tallied across all 26 fields per country.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Manual cross-validation}
\label{subsec:methods-crossval}
Because errors in OpenAlex's underlying author disambiguation or citation counts would propagate directly into every successive index, we manually spot-check a targeted, non-random sample of authors rather than a random one, chosen at the points where an error would be both most likely and most consequential: authors sitting exactly at an institution's h\textsubscript{2} threshold, where a $\pm 1$ error in a single h\textsubscript{1} could change the institution's rank; top authors in fields where OpenAlex's topic classification is known to be thinner (e.g., Arts and Humanities); top authors at institutions with non-Anglophone naming conventions, where author-name disambiguation is more error-prone and where OpenAlex's coverage is known to diverge unevenly from other databases by country, most severely for China~\citep{zheng2025}, including institutions suspected of affiliation-swapping for grant purposes~\citep{saudi}; and the small number of authors with the highest h\textsubscript{1} globally, as a sanity check at the extreme of the distribution. For each candidate we resolve the author's OpenAlex profile through the public API and compare it by hand against Web of Science, Scopus, and Google Scholar.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Manual cross-validation}
\label{subsec:crossval-results}
This subsection's comparisons were made by hand against an earlier pull of the OpenAlex snapshot and have not been redone against the current one described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}; given the scale of drift documented in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}, the specific h\textsubscript{1} values quoted below should be read as illustrating the kind and rough magnitude of disagreement across sources, not as current figures for these particular authors. Of the 19 authors sampled under the scheme described in Subsection~\ref{subsec:methods-crossval}, Table~\ref{tab:crossval} summarizes agreement between OpenAlex's \texttt{h\_index} and the corresponding value we located by hand in Web of Science, Scopus, and Google Scholar. Coverage itself varies sharply: Scopus returned a value for 16 of the 19 authors, Google Scholar for 12, and Web of Science for only 11.

Scopus tracks OpenAlex most closely, both in coverage and in magnitude of disagreement. Google Scholar is systematically higher than OpenAlex, consistent with its broader crawl of preprints, theses, and other grey literature and its comparative permissiveness toward self-citation; this ordering (Google Scholar $>$ Scopus $>$ OpenAlex $>$ Web of Science) reproduces cleanly among the five global top-h\textsubscript{1} sanity-check authors, none of whom show a discrepancy exceeding 27\% of their OpenAlex value in either direction.

Four authors, however, show disagreement well outside this range. We read this as a signature of author-disambiguation failure (multiple distinct people merged under one OpenAlex author ID) rather than of genuine cross-database disagreement:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Donald Small} (Johns Hopkins): $\mathrm{h}_1^{\mathrm{OpenAlex}}=103$ against Scopus $=57$ and Google Scholar $=59$.
    \item \textbf{Karen S.\ Calhoun} (University of Michigan): $\mathrm{h}_1^{\mathrm{OpenAlex}}=45$ against Scopus $=11$ and Google Scholar $=13$.
    \item \textbf{Lei Jiang} (University of Science and Technology of China): $\mathrm{h}_1^{\mathrm{OpenAlex}}=215$ against Web of Science $=115$, consistent with documented China-specific coverage discrepancies in OpenAlex~\citep{zheng2025}.
    \item \textbf{A.\ Jafari} (Sharif University of Technology): $\mathrm{h}_1^{\mathrm{OpenAlex}}=139$, but Web of Science, Scopus, and Google Scholar report $41$, $133$, and $224$ respectively.
\end{itemize}
All four fall in the non-Anglophone-institution or common-name strata of the sample, matching the concern raised in Subsection~\ref{subsec:methods-crossval} that disambiguation is more error-prone for exactly these authors. Because such merges can only inflate an author's apparent $\mathrm{h}_1$, and $\mathrm{h}_1$ feeds directly into $\mathrm{h}_2$ and $\mathrm{h}_3$, a handful of merged identities at the right institution could measurably distort an institution- or country-level ranking; we flag this as an open limitation rather than one this sample can size definitively.

The two Arts and Humanities threshold authors sampled (Gillian Ramchand and G.\ Herbert Fowler, both sitting at Oxford's field-specific $\mathrm{h}_2=30$ threshold) returned no value in any of the three external sources. Citation indexes are well documented to under-represent book-based humanities scholarship relative to journal-based fields~\citep{nederhof2006}; these two null results are consistent with OpenAlex inheriting the same gap, though our sample is too small to confirm it directly. Either way, h-index comparisons in these fields should be read with correspondingly less confidence than in the sciences.

\subsection{Institution efficiency and the $\beta_1$ scaling law}
\label{subsec:h2-efficiency}
Section~\ref{subsec:methods-supplementary} derived $\mathrm{h}_2 = \text{author\_count}^{1/\beta_1}$ from Egghe's eq.\ 11 with $\beta_1=1.833$ fit by discrete MLE. Figure~\ref{fig:h2-vs-size} plots this relationship directly: a regression of h\textsubscript{2} on $\text{author\_count}^{1/\beta_1}$, forced through the origin, across all 18,569 institutions with at least 10 authors gives slope $0.229$ and $R^2=0.809$ — a somewhat weaker fit than the $\sqrt{N}$ heuristic it replaces ($R^2=0.88$), visibly so at the largest institutions in Figure~\ref{fig:h2-vs-size}, where the fitted line runs above the bulk of the data. $\beta_1=1.833$ is, after all, an empirical tail exponent of the h\textsubscript{1} distribution pooled across every field and career stage in OpenAlex, not a free parameter tuned to make this particular regression look good; Section~\ref{subsec:model-consistency} shows that same pooling is exactly what makes $\beta_1$ an imperfect description of any single, more homogeneous sub-population.

\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/h2_vs_size.png}

\subsection{Testing the compounding-exponent model}
\label{subsec:model-consistency}
Section~\ref{subsec:methods-supplementary} noted that the four exponents fit globally are inconsistent with Egghe's own model: $\beta_1/\alpha_1$ and $\beta_2/(\alpha_1\alpha_2)$ both imply $\alpha_0<1$, which his eq.\ 4 rules out. Egghe's derivation assumes a single, homogeneous Lotka process at each level; fitting globally pools together every OpenAlex field's citation practices at once, from Medicine to Arts and Humanities, which is a very different population from the one his model describes. To test whether this pooling is what breaks the model, we refit all four exponents restricted to a single field at a time. Table~\ref{tab:model-consistency} shows the result for Medicine (the largest field, 6.80 million authors) and Physics and Astronomy (a mid-sized field, 756,898 authors, culturally a very different citation regime from Medicine).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Implied $\alpha_0=\beta_1/\alpha_1$ and the $\beta_2$ vs.\ $\beta_1\alpha_2$ compounding check, global vs.\ within a single field. A model-consistent fit requires implied $\alpha_0>1$ and $\beta_2 \approx \beta_1\alpha_2$.}
\label{tab:model-consistency}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrrr@{}}
\toprule
& Global (all fields) & Medicine & Physics and Astronomy \\
\midrule
Implied $\alpha_0=\beta_1/\alpha_1$ & 0.612 & 0.890 & 0.981 \\
$\beta_1$ vs.\ power law & favored & exponential favored & exponential favored \\
$\beta_2$ vs.\ $\beta_1\alpha_2$ predicted & $-21\%$ & $-42\%$ & $-45\%$ \\
$\beta_2$ vs.\ power law & exponential favored & exponential favored & exponential favored \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The two rows tell different stories. Implied $\alpha_0$ moves steadily toward the model's required boundary as the population narrows: $0.612$ globally, $0.890$ within Medicine, $0.981$ within Physics and Astronomy, essentially consistent with $\alpha_0=1$ once field heterogeneity is controlled for. That supports treating the author-level violation as an artifact of pooling citation practices from every field into one fit, not a flaw in the compounding logic itself. The $\beta_2$ row does not resolve the same way: the mismatch between observed $\beta_2$ and the model's predicted $\beta_1\alpha_2$ does not shrink within a single field, and in both fields tested the h\textsubscript{2}-across-institutions distribution is more consistent with an exponential tail than a power-law one -- a result that recurs identically at the global level. Unlike the author-level story, this looks like a genuine break in Egghe's model specifically at the institution-aggregation step (his eq.\ 14), not an artifact of heterogeneity. We use the empirically fit $\beta_1$ and $\beta_2$ in the efficiency metrics regardless: Egghe's eq.\ 11 and eq.\ 18 functional forms are a defensible normalization independent of whether this deeper multiplicative-consistency check holds, and Figures~\ref{fig:h2-vs-size} and~\ref{fig:h3-vs-size} show both fits are still reasonable descriptions of h\textsubscript{2} and h\textsubscript{3} as functions of group size, whatever the shape of the underlying tail.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Schubert closed his 2007 note wondering whether h\textsubscript{2} of institutes would prove more interesting than h\textsubscript{2} of journals, absent the researcher-institute-country data needed to check. Nineteen years and 27.1 million OpenAlex authors later, the answer is yes, but not in the way a single top-10 table would suggest: the overall ranking in Table~\ref{tab:overall-h2} turns out to be substantially a ranking of biomedical author-pool size (Subsection~\ref{subsec:nonbiomedical}, where even Harvard's own \#1 position does not survive excluding biomedical fields), and it is only once h\textsubscript{2} is split by field (Subsection~\ref{subsec:field-level}) that the successive index proves valuable, surfacing institutions such as Wageningen, Tohoku, and the University of Bologna as world leaders in specific disciplines invisible to any university-wide metric. Egghe's~\citeyearpar{egghe2008} compounding-exponent model gives the efficiency metrics of Subsections~\ref{subsec:h2-efficiency} and~\ref{subsec:h3-efficiency} real theoretical footing in place of the $\sqrt{N}$/$\log_2 N$ heuristics of an earlier version of this analysis: both $\mathrm{h}_2=\text{author\_count}^{1/\beta_1}$ and $\mathrm{h}_3=\text{institution\_count}^{1/\beta_2}$ use exponents fit directly from the data by discrete MLE rather than assumed, and both hold reasonably well ($R^2=0.809$ and $0.937$ respectively) across several orders of magnitude in group size. Subsection~\ref{subsec:model-consistency}'s direct test of Egghe's model went further than the efficiency metrics strictly required and found a genuinely mixed result: the model's author-level consistency requirement, violated when fit globally, is restored almost exactly once field heterogeneity is controlled for, while its institution-level requirement is not, a specific and reproducible boundary on where a 2008 Lotkaian model of citation aggregation still holds against 2026 data.

None of this is airtight, and one limitation is not of the usual kind. \texttt{s3://openalex/data/parquet/authors/} is a live, continuously-updated snapshot, not a versioned release, and partway through this analysis we had to fully re-pull it to add one column (\texttt{works\_count}, needed to fit $\alpha_1$). That re-pull did not reproduce the authors snapshot an earlier version of this paper was computed from: overall author count fell from roughly 30 million to 27.1 million, Harvard's institution-level h\textsubscript{2} fell from 137 to 71 on a similarly reduced author count (92,274 vs.\ 110,979 previously), and, more strikingly, several field-level claims reversed outright rather than merely shifting -- University of Antwerp no longer leads Physics and Astronomy, the University of Chinese Academy of Sciences no longer leads Chemical Engineering, and China's Academic Olympics gold count fell from 5 to 2 (Subsection~\ref{subsec:olympics}), though three of those five field-level races are decided by margins of one or two institutions and could plausibly flip again on the next pull. We cannot fully disentangle how much of this is OpenAlex correcting author-disambiguation errors of exactly the kind Subsection~\ref{subsec:crossval-results} independently flags (huge, implausible \texttt{works\_count} values paired with single-digit h-indices, which we excluded via a fixed cutoff before fitting $\alpha_1$ in Subsection~\ref{subsec:methods-supplementary}) versus ordinary snapshot growth versus some other backend change, since OpenAlex does not version its public snapshot in a way that lets us diff the two states directly. Whatever the cause, every number in this paper is a snapshot of a moving target, and a live, continuously-updated source is a genuine hazard to point-in-time bibliometric claims at this level of aggregation, not just a hypothetical one: we could reproduce this fact but not the specific numbers it produced eighteen months from now with the identical pipeline.

The cross-validation in Subsection~\ref{subsec:crossval-results} (against an earlier pull, per the note there) found four of nineteen sampled authors with h\textsubscript{1} discrepancies large enough to suggest author-disambiguation error rather than genuine cross-database disagreement and concentrated exactly where expected: non-Anglophone institutions and common names. Because such errors propagate upward by construction, a handful of merged identities at the right institution could shift an h\textsubscript{2} or h\textsubscript{3} ranking, and we have no way to size that effect from a sample chosen for likely error rather than representativeness. Coverage gaps likely compound this in fields like Arts and Humanities, where citation databases are well documented to under-represent book-based scholarship~\citep{nederhof2006} and our own cross-validation sample turned up no external value at all for either Arts and Humanities author checked. A natural next step would be to reassign h\textsubscript{2} to apply to departments rather than universities, once OpenAlex or another database provides such data. Then, h\textsubscript{3} could be calculated at the university level, and h\textsubscript{4} at the country level. The results here still seem, in Schubert's own terms, conceivable but not bluntly trivial: a positive sign for the successive h-index as a coherent frame for multi-level research assessment, nineteen years and several orders of magnitude of data on from where he left it -- provided the specific numbers are read as a snapshot, not a fixed measurement.
